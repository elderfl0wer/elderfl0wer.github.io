LPM

A lightweight password manager for the command line.

Synopsis

LPM allows you to manage your passwords via terminal and encrypts them using Libsodium & uses an identity system to keep track of passwords.

History

Quite literally stands for "Lightweight Password Manager", months before the project began I had discovered Libsodium and was mostly intrigued by its randombytes API so I wanted to use it for months but never actually got around to it.

The philosophy is that a password is something that can be remembered by a human being i.e. not being hash-like, but to make a strong password it has to be complex hence becoming hard to remember. LPM circumvents that by allowing you to have complex passwords for logins you wish while only having to memorize passwords to your "identities".

The identity system of LPM works to save your passwords into seperate storage units that are identified via an "identity name" & optionally an EMAIL and/or Phone No. which has a password associated with it which needs to be verified by LPM to add, search or extract a password from that Identity.

Furthermore, each identity EACH have their own password and allows clean seperation between the types of passwords saved for example you can have your personal logins under an identity with your name associated with your personal EMAIL & number while another one for work associated with a work email & number each having different passwords and do not interact with each other I.E. the login for work will not show up if querying the personal identity as they are stored in different encrypted files & under different passwords i.e. they cannot affect each other.

This also means you own more of your data, since the passwords are not stored in some cloud but rather in encrypted storage units on your machine, you are not vulnerable to data leaks or privacy invasion as YOU OWN YOUR DATA by keeping it on your machine.
